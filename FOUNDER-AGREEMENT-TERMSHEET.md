## 3. Equity split  ← the hard conversation
Proposed split: Eric ____% / Partner ____%

Things to weigh honestly (there is no "fair" number — only an *agreed* number):
- **Building the entire product solo** is enormous, ongoing, high-risk labor. Code is the thing that has to keep working forever.
- **A trusted distribution channel into the exact buyer** is rare and is often *the* reason a company survives. An audience of actual barbershop owners is worth real equity.
- "Came up with the idea" is worth **very little** on its own — ideas are cheap, execution is everything. Don't let idea-origin drive the split; let *ongoing contribution* drive it.
- Whoever is doing more of the *continuing* work (not the one-time spark) should get more.

A 50/50 is common between true equal co-founders but is also the classic deadlock trap. 60/40 or similar with a clear CEO is often healthier. **Decide on contribution, not friendship.**

## 4. Vesting  ← the clause that saves the company
**All founder equity vests over 4 years with a 1-year cliff.** Meaning:
- If a founder leaves before 1 year → they get **0%**.
- After 1 year → 25% vests, then monthly for the next 3 years.
- **Why:** this protects BOTH of you. If your partner posts twice and disappears, he doesn't walk away owning half of what you spent years building. If you bail, same applies to you. Without vesting, the person who quits early keeps a dead-weight stake forever and makes the company un-runnable and un-investable.
- This is non-negotiable standard practice. Anyone who refuses vesting is telling you they might leave.

## 5. Concrete commitments (turn "ready to do content" into something real)
Vague promises are how partnerships rot. Write down what each person actually commits to in the first 90 days:

Partner (distribution):
- ⬜ ____ posts/pieces of content by ____
- ⬜ Warm intros to ____ barbershop owners by ____
- ⬜ Convert ____ paying shops by ____

Eric (product):
- ⬜ SMS live within ____ days of Twilio A2P approval
- ⬜ Onboarding flow a barber can complete in < ____ minutes
- ⬜ ____

> Review these at 90 days. If someone isn't delivering, vesting + roles let you fix it without a war.

## 6. IP assignment
- All code, designs, accounts, domains, and content created for ChairBack belong to **the company**, not to either individual. Both sign an IP assignment so nobody can walk away with the codebase or the domain. (Right now the repo + getchairback.com are effectively Eric's personally — this fixes that once an entity exists.)

## 7. Money
- Revenue goes to a **company** bank account, not a personal one.
- Founders take **no salary** until ____ (e.g. a revenue threshold you both agree on).
- Expenses (Twilio, Stripe fees, hosting, domain) are company expenses, tracked from day one.
- Any profit distribution follows the equity split, decided by the CEO/both.

## 8. What happens if someone leaves
- Vesting (section 4) governs the equity they keep.
- The company can buy back unvested (or even vested) shares at ____ (fair value / formula).
- A departing founder cannot poach customers or rebuild a clone for ____ months (non-compete — keep it reasonable/enforceable).

## 9. Decision-making
- Day-to-day: each owner decides within their area (section 2).
- Big stuff (raising money, selling the company, taking on debt, big spend over $____): requires ⬜ both ⬜ CEO + sign-off.
